The 75% Attendance Rule: A Broken Compass for Modern Education

Does forcing students into classrooms prepare them for the future, or hold them back?

The education system measures attendance, but it misses the real point of learning: curiosity, skills, and innovation.

The Problem with Mandatory Attendance

Let’s talk about two types of students:

  • Ambitious learners: Innovators, project builders, and self-driven explorers.
  • Unfocused learners: Those still discovering their goals and passions.
Figure 1: Ambitious vs. Unfocused learners under rigid attendance policies.

Impact on Ambitious Students

Ambitious students thrive when given freedom to explore projects, internships, and real-world opportunities. The 75% rule, however:

  • Forces them to waste hours in unengaging classrooms.
  • Limits their ability to build skills beyond textbooks.
  • Frustrates those who want to innovate and experiment.

The Unfocused Learners: A Missed Opportunity

For those still figuring out their goals, forced attendance is not the solution. What they truly need:

  • Mentorship from real-world role models.
  • Exposure to success stories that inspire them.
  • Time to explore at their own pace.
Figure 2: Compulsion frustrates ambitious and uninspired students alike.

Why Does the Rule Exist?

  • For parents: It creates an illusion of productivity and safety.
  • For institutions: It guarantees revenue and standardized metrics.

The reality? The rule prioritizes compliance over capability.

What’s the Real Solution?

To fix this broken system, we need a new approach:

  • Allow ambitious students the freedom to innovate, experiment, and grow.
  • Provide unfocused learners with mentorship, guidance, and real-world inspiration.
  • Shift the focus from attendance to outcomes like skills, curiosity, and personal growth.
Figure 3: Freedom and purpose drive meaningful education.

Redefining Success

The 75% rule belongs to an outdated system. Education must evolve to:

  • Measure engagement, not just presence.
  • Foster curiosity and growth.
  • Prepare students for success, not just attendance records.

Are we preparing students for attendance or for a successful future?
